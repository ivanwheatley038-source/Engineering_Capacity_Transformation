# Engineering Standards

## Objective

As engineering capacity increased, maintaining consistency across multiple teams became as important as increasing delivery throughput.

Rather than allowing each team to develop independent delivery practices, a common set of engineering standards was established to create consistency, improve quality, simplify onboarding, and enable leaders to scale effectively.

These standards became reusable organizational assets that supported every engineering team throughout the software delivery lifecycle.

---

# Engineering Operating Standards

The standards established a common framework for planning, executing, and completing engineering work.

Each artifact was intentionally designed to reduce variation while preserving team autonomy and engineering creativity.

The result was a consistent delivery experience regardless of product area or delivery team.

---

# Core Standards

## Definition of Ready

A standardized checklist used during backlog refinement to ensure work was sufficiently understood before entering active development.

Objectives

- Improve planning quality
- Reduce mid-sprint uncertainty
- Improve estimation accuracy
- Increase engineering confidence

---

## Definition of Done

A common completion standard defining when work could be considered complete.

Objectives

- Establish consistent quality expectations
- Improve release readiness
- Reduce production defects
- Increase delivery consistency

---

## Project Intake

A standardized intake process for evaluating new requests before engineering work began.

Objectives

- Improve work prioritization
- Capture required business information
- Identify dependencies early
- Increase planning efficiency

---

## Solution Design & Estimation

A repeatable framework used to analyze proposed work, evaluate implementation approaches, estimate effort, and identify delivery risks.

Objectives

- Improve estimate consistency
- Increase planning accuracy
- Identify technical complexity
- Improve architectural alignment

---

## Sprint Closure Reporting

A standardized reporting process used at the conclusion of each sprint to communicate delivery outcomes, accomplishments, risks, and follow-up actions.

Objectives

- Improve delivery transparency
- Standardize leadership reporting
- Measure team performance
- Support continuous improvement

---

# Benefits

Standardizing engineering practices created organizational advantages beyond individual projects.

The standards enabled:

- Consistent planning across teams
- Faster onboarding of new engineers
- Predictable engineering execution
- Improved communication
- Reduced process variation
- Greater delivery transparency
- Increased organizational scalability

---

# Relationship to the Operating Model

The Engineering Standards provided the repeatable assets used throughout the Operating Model.

While the Operating Model defined **how work flowed through the organization**, these standards defined **how engineering teams executed each stage of that work**.

Together they created a consistent engineering system capable of supporting sustained organizational growth.

# Leadership Principles

Several principles guided the development of these standards.

- Standardize quality—not creativity.
- Create reusable assets that scale.
- Reduce ambiguity through clear expectations.
- Build repeatable systems instead of individual heroics.
- Continuously improve through inspection and adaptation.

---

# Outcome

The Engineering Standards became a foundational component of the organization's delivery system.

Combined with organizational redesign and a common operating model, these standards enabled multiple engineering teams to execute consistently, onboard new team members efficiently, and maintain quality while substantially increasing engineering delivery capacity over multiple years.
